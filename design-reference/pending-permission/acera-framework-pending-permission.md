## The ACERA Framework

ACERA is a five-step process for structuring a coaching conversation. The acronym stands for:

**A**gree → **C**omprehend → **E**xplore → **R**ecap → **A**ct

---

### Step 1: Agree

**What it is:** The agreement is the outcome the client wants from the session. It is, in essence, what the client wants to know or accomplish by the end of the conversation. It is the most important step of the coaching conversation because it gives the conversation direction and purpose.

**Key Points:**

- It must be focused and specific enough to coach around in the given time frame. If it is vague or broad, coaching will be ineffective.
- The coach's job is to get the client to a specific, measurable aim for the session. This may require asking further questions to help the client narrow the topic.
- The coach's job is to keep the agreement top of mind so that the session doesn't veer far off track. This is the coach's responsibility, not the client's.
- If the client doesn't get what they want, even if it's an interesting, thought-provoking conversation, eventually they will be dissatisfied and feel something is missing. If this happens repeatedly, the client may move on from coaching.
- At the end of the session, the coach should check in to see if the agreement was met. Review it mentally and verbally and compare the outcome of the session to the agreement of the session. Did you deliver?

**Potential Pitfalls:** The biggest pitfall in the first step is not getting an agreement that is specific and narrow enough. When this happens, it is difficult to create breakthroughs, develop powerful questions that get to the essence of the matter, and guide the client to the most effective solutions.

---

### Step 2: Comprehend

**What it is:** The purpose of the comprehension phase is to define the obstacles and challenges to achieving the desired outcome. There is a *gap* between where the client is and where they want to be. Comprehension articulates the gap and empowers the coach and client to strategically explore possible solutions.

*Definition of obstacle: fear, resources, people, thoughts, habits, words — any challenge that gets in the way of closing the gap.*

**Key Points:**

- In the midst of comprehension, acknowledge the problem, acknowledge the client's recognition of it, and acknowledge what they have already done about it. This opens them up to go deeper because they feel understood. A person who feels understood will want to keep going.
- Notice the kind of words the client uses to give clues about the questions to ask.
- If there is anything the client is saying that you do not understand, say so. Ask questions to help you better understand the problem.
- Articulate both the *dilemma* and the *desire*. There's a reason the client needs coaching on the subject at hand.
- Explore beliefs, thoughts, and habits that are blocking the desired outcome. *(Example: people know how to lose weight, but there are reasons they are not doing it.)*
- Explore fears, obstacles, and what's holding the client back.

**Potential Pitfalls:** Many coaches want to go straight to *what to do* rather than exploring the obstacles that have kept the client from doing it. Comprehending the obstacles gives a concrete focus to explore a solution that removes or reduces the obstacles and makes it possible for the client to move toward the desired outcome.

---

### Step 3: Explore

**What it is:** The purpose of the exploration phase is to identify multiple options for a solution, explore the obstacles to a solution, and begin to narrow those options. Exploration is the most powerful phase for transformation in the coaching conversation — this is where 90% of the "aha" moments happen.

**Key Points:**

- Exploring is just that — *exploration*. It is not commitment. Remind the client of this. Sometimes clients are afraid to acknowledge solutions that take them out of their comfort zone, evoke fear, or seem unreasonable. Acknowledging such options can lead to creative solutions.
- Explore options to move toward a solution.
- Remember that *identifying* options is different from *committing* to an option.
- This is an opportunity to redefine beliefs, choose new thoughts, and design new habits — examining thoughts, triggers, and reactions.
- Sometimes this turns a "conversational session" into a "working session." The coach might have the client take action on a challenge right during the session — for example, the client might draft a paragraph of a memo they've been procrastinating about writing, make a critical phone call and call back, or send an email that the coach and client craft together.
- Ask: is there a behavior/thought/habit redefinition that is helpful? If not, return to exploring some more.

**Potential Pitfalls:** It can be tempting to want to give the client the answer. Ask questions that help the client identify potential answers before making suggestions. One of the coach's jobs is to stretch the client beyond their normal thinking and comfort zone. Ask for multiple options, especially when there only seems to be one option.

---

### Step 4: Recap

**What it is:** In the Recap step, identify the most important points and takeaways from the session. Preferably, have the client articulate the recap, as the highlights that resonate most with the client may actually differ from the coach's perception. However, time does not always permit, and it is acceptable for the coach to recap for the client by stating the key takeaways from the session.

**Key Points:**

- Encourage the client to *write it down*.
- Suggest the client keep a coaching session journal. This becomes a record of the wisdom they are gaining and how they are growing through the process of coaching.
- It also helps the client and coach articulate how coaching is helping them.

---

### Step 5: Act

**What it is:** No session is complete without the client committing to a clear course of action after the session ends and before the next session. In the *Explore* phase, the client identified options for moving forward. Now, in the *Act* phase, the coach helps the client decide which course of action to take, when they will take it, and what resources will help them ensure a successful outcome.

**Key Points:**

- Beware of *goal fatigue*. Do not overwhelm the client with too many action steps. Determine the number and type of actions based on the amount of time between sessions, the time each action will take, and the client's schedule. Typically, 2–4 commitments between sessions are doable.
- Help the client celebrate wins. Articulate and acknowledge milestones. Clients often take their own strides and efforts for granted — point them out and help them savor progress.
- Sometimes the client will have a hard time pinpointing the right next step. Stretch and challenge them with actions that give them an opportunity to muster courage in the face of their fears and move closer to their authentic vision.

**Potential Pitfalls:** Do not move too quickly to action steps before comprehending all obstacles and exploring solutions. Doing so may lead to actions that do not produce lasting results or address the core issues that must be addressed in order to make sustainable change.
